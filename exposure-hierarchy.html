Exposure Hierarchy: The Fear Ladder

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Exposure therapy helps reduce fear and avoidance by approaching the situations, sensations, or memories that trigger anxiety — gradually and repeatedly, in a planned way. Over time, the trigger feels less powerful and you gain confidence in your ability to cope. This worksheet helps you build your personal fear ladder and plan your exposures step by step.

🧠 New Learning

Your brain learns that the feared outcome is less likely — or more manageable — than it predicted. Each exposure rewrites the threat message.

💪 Confidence Through Action

Each approach step shows you that you can cope, which reduces reliance on avoidance and safety habits that keep anxiety going.

🔍 Attention Shift

You begin to notice the full picture rather than only the threat cue — breaking the cycle of hypervigilance and anxiety.

🪜 Gradual Steps

Starting with easier situations and working up the ladder builds momentum — each step making the next one more manageable.

How Exposure Works

Follow these five steps each time you do an exposure. Over time, this process retrains your brain's threat response.

1

Define the Target

Name the feared cue or situation as clearly and specifically as you can. Vague targets make it hard to know when you have succeeded.

2

Predict

Write down the feared outcome and how intense you expect the anxiety to feel (using SUDS 0–100). This gives you something concrete to test against reality.

3

Approach on Purpose

Enter the situation and stay long enough for learning to occur. Leaving too quickly can reinforce the fear — aim to stay until anxiety reduces or levels off.

4

Drop Safety Behaviours

Reduce behaviours that hide real learning — such as constant reassurance seeking, distraction, or carrying safety objects. Your brain needs to experience coping without them.

5

Repeat and Review

Practise across different places and times until fear reduces and confidence grows. Record your SUDS before, during, and after each exposure to track progress.

💡 What to expect: Anxiety usually rises first, then levels or drops as you stay in the situation. The goal is learning, not forcing the feeling to disappear. You may feel uncomfortable — that is expected and normal. The discomfort is temporary; the learning is lasting.
