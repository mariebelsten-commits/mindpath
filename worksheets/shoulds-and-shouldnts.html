How to use this worksheet

Start by listing all of the "should," "must," and "ought" statements you notice yourself making — about yourself, about others, and about life. Then work through each section, taking your time with the questions. There are no right or wrong answers — the goal is awareness and flexibility, not perfection.

1
Identify Your "Should" Statements

Write down all of the "should," "must," and "ought" rules you notice yourself holding. Don't filter or judge — just list them as they come. Include rules you apply to yourself, to others, and to life in general.

I should always be productive I must never show weakness Others should treat me fairly Life ought to be fair I shouldn't need help
↓
2
The Challenge

Choose the most demanding "should" statement from your list above — the one that feels the most rigid or the most distressing. Write it below, then work through the questions that follow.

Questions to explore Where does this belief come from? Who says you must follow it? Is there any rule that says you have to? Why does it matter so much to you?
↓
3
Cost–Benefit Analysis

List the advantages and disadvantages of holding onto this belief or rule. Be honest — there may be real advantages, and that is worth acknowledging.

↓
4
Try a Substitute

Rewrite your "should" statement using a softer, more flexible phrase. The goal is not to abandon your values — it is to hold them more gently, without turning them into rigid demands.

"I would feel better if..." "I would prefer it if..." "I would feel more comfortable if..." "It would be nice if..."
↓
5
"Should" Busting — Daily Practice

Spend five minutes each day writing down or saying aloud all of the "should," "must," and "ought" statements you notice. Over time, this practice helps you recognise just how many unhelpful and unrealistic demands you place on yourself and others — and how automatic they have become.

Reflection After trying this practice, what did you notice? Were there more "should" thoughts than you expected? Did any of them surprise you?
🌿 Remember: "Should" statements are not facts — they are rules you have learned, often long ago, in a very different context. Questioning them is not weakness. It is one of the most courageous and compassionate things you can do for yourself.
