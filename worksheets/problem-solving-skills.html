Problem-Solving Skills

When problems feel overwhelming, breaking them into smaller steps makes them easier to manage. Define the issue clearly, explore solutions, and plan your best next step.

🎯

🔍

✅

Take Action

Weigh up the options and choose the one that feels most realistic and helpful right now

How to use this worksheet

Work through each step in order. Start by describing the problem in a single, clear sentence — keep it specific. Then break it apart, brainstorm as many solutions as you can think of (even imperfect ones), weigh up the options, and finally choose the one step you will try first. There are no right or wrong answers — the goal is to move from feeling stuck to feeling capable.

1
Define the Problem

Write one clear sentence describing the problem. Try to be as specific as possible — vague problems are harder to solve.

I am often late paying bills I keep putting off a difficult conversation I feel overwhelmed at work and don't know where to start
↓
2
Break It Down

What makes this problem difficult? Consider all the factors involved — unhelpful thoughts, difficult emotions, behaviours you notice yourself doing (or avoiding), and any practical obstacles.

Prompts to consider What thoughts come up when you think about this problem? What feelings does it bring up? What do you find yourself doing — or not doing — because of it? Are there any practical barriers (time, money, energy, support)?
↓
3
Brainstorm Solutions

Write down as many ideas as you can — don't filter or judge them yet. Even solutions that seem impractical or silly are worth noting, as they can lead to better ones.

Choose a Solution

Which option seems best right now? You don't need to find the perfect solution — just the most helpful one you can realistically try. Write what you will do and when.

Things to consider Which option has the best balance of pros and cons? What feels manageable to try first? Even a small step in the right direction counts.
🌱 Remember: Problem-solving is a skill, and like any skill it gets easier with practice. You don't need to solve everything at once — taking one small, thoughtful step is always enough to begin.
