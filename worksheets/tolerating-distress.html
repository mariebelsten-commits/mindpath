How It Works

Understanding distress tolerance

Pain and distress are part of life — they cannot be entirely avoided or removed. Always trying to escape pain leads to more problems than it solves. Distress tolerance isn't about feeling better instantly; it's about getting through the moment without making things worse.

1

Accept that pain is part of life

Attempts to shut out emotional pain — through avoidance, alcohol, or self-harm — come back to haunt us. People with PTSD or pathological grief are primarily trying to avoid cues that cause discomfort. The goal is to feel without being destroyed by it. The more you tolerate distress, the stronger you get.

2

Rate your distress using SUDS

The Subjective Units of Distress Scale (SUDS) runs from 1 (completely calm) to 10 (highest possible distress). Rating yourself gives you a snapshot of where you are right now — and helps you choose the strategy that matches your level.

3

Spot your personal warning signs

Warning signs are the thoughts, feelings, and body sensations that tell you distress is building. Getting to know them early means you can use coping strategies sooner — before things become overwhelming.

4

Match your strategy to your distress level

Different skills work best at different intensities. When distress is very high (8–10), body-based strategies like TIPP work best — your thinking brain is less available. At lower levels, distancing, communication, and skills like Radical Acceptance become more accessible.

5

Know your safety plan in advance

If distress ever becomes unsafe, a pre-written safety plan tells you what to do step by step. You don't have to think clearly under pressure — you just follow the plan. Review it with your therapist so you both know what it says.

Radical Acceptance
💡 About the strategies: These are skills drawn from Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT). TIPP (Temperature, Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation) and STOP work by changing your physiology directly when distress is highest. ACCEPTS uses distraction. Self-Soothing engages the five senses. IMPROVE and Pros & Cons work when you have more cognitive capacity. Radical Acceptance means fully accepting reality as it is — without fighting it. Your therapist can teach you each of these in more depth.

🌿 Remember
