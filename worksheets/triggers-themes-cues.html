Identifying Triggers, Themes & Cues

Learn what sets off your strongest reactions, capture those moments clearly, and group them into themes you can plan for and work with.

What this worksheet does

A trigger is any cue — external or internal — that quickly sets off a noticeable change in your thoughts, feelings, body signals, or behaviour. This worksheet has three parts: first, you map your personal triggers by sense and situation; then you log a specific trigger episode in detail; finally, you use the Theme Builder to group repeated patterns into a named theme and plan a better response.

Regular use builds self-awareness fast. Even one entry a week reveals patterns that are invisible in the moment.

Part A — My Triggers

Common trigger types

  • Situations: crowds, queues, confined spaces, driving, meetings
  • People & relationships: conflict, criticism, authority figures, separation
  • Sensory cues: loud sounds, particular smells, touch, visual reminders
  • Body states: poor sleep, hunger, caffeine, illness, pain, hormone shifts
  • Internal cues: memories, images, worries, self-talk, perfection rules
  • Times & places: late at night, early morning, certain locations or routes

Part B — Trigger Log Entry

How to use the trigger log

Fill this in as soon as possible after a trigger episode — while the details are fresh. Keep entries short and concrete. Use context tags (1–3 per entry) with consistent wording so you can spot patterns over time. For example, always write "Work" rather than sometimes "work" and sometimes "office".

Part C — Theme Builder

What is a theme?

A theme is a repeating pattern that links several triggers — for example, "criticism at work", "being rushed", or "social evaluation". Naming themes helps you plan supports and skills ahead of time rather than reacting in the moment. Group similar triggers into two or three words you can scan quickly. Use the same wording each time.
