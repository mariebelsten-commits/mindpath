Theory A vs Theory B

Anxiety often rests on a hidden assumption about what the real problem is. This worksheet helps you put two competing theories side by side — and discover which one the evidence actually supports.

🔬

Two Explanations

Theory A and Theory B offer competing accounts of the same experience. Only one can be right — or at least, one fits the evidence better.

⚖️

Weigh the Evidence

What actually supports each theory? Looking at real evidence helps you move beyond gut feelings and anxious assumptions.

🎯

Consider the Implications

If each theory were true, what would you need to do? The answer often reveals which theory is keeping your anxiety going.

🔮

Look to the Future

Which theory, if true, would open up a better life? Seeing the future implications can be powerfully motivating.

How It Works

A step-by-step guide

Work through each section for both theories before moving to the next. Try to be as honest and specific as you can.

1

Understand the two theories

Theory A is usually the anxiety-maintaining belief — it frames the problem as an external threat or a real danger that must be managed. Theory B reframes the problem: the anxiety itself is the issue, not the feared thing. Theory B opens the door to different, more helpful responses.

2

State both theories clearly

Write each theory as a simple statement. Theory A often begins "My problem is that there is real danger/illness/risk..." Theory B often begins "My problem is that I am anxious about..."

3

Gather evidence for each

Write down any evidence that supports each theory. Try to be specific and honest — the evidence column often reveals that Theory B is better supported than it first appears.

4

Consider what each theory requires

If Theory A were true, what would you need to do? If Theory B were true, what then? The actions each theory demands can reveal a lot about which one is actually driving your behaviour.

5

Reflect on the future

What would each theory mean for you and your future if it turned out to be true? This question helps you see the long-term consequences of living by each theory.

Theory A — Example
"My problem is that I have a serious undetected illness. My anxiety is a reasonable response to a genuine health risk, and I need to keep checking to stay safe."
Theory B — Example
"My problem is that I am highly anxious about my health. My body's normal sensations are being misinterpreted as dangerous. The anxiety — not the illness — is what I need to address."
💡 Tip: Theory B is not about dismissing your concerns — it's about asking whether there might be a more accurate explanation for what you're experiencing. Working with your therapist on this exercise gives it extra power.
