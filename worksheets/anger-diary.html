Anger Diary

Anger builds silently, outside of awareness. This diary helps you spot your personal triggers and warning signs — so you can catch your anger while it's still small enough to manage.

How to use this diary

Anger has a tendency to build like the hum of a refrigerator — you can hear it, but you don't notice it until it's grown too big to manage. Keeping an Anger Diary helps you understand the pattern: what sets your anger off, what it looks and feels like as it builds, how you react, and what happens as a result.

Start by completing Part 1 (your personal warning signs) and Part 2 (your anger thermometer). These help you build self-awareness before using the diary. Then complete a diary entry after each anger episode. After a few entries, review them for patterns using the Reflection section at the end.

Term
Definition
Examples
Trigger
Something that sets off your anger
Getting laughed at, dirty dishes left out, a dismissive comment, not getting a job offer
Warning Signs
The feelings, sensations, and behaviours that tell you your anger is building
Feeling irritated, clenching your fists, wanting to argue, raising your voice, getting defensive
Response
Your reaction when your anger is at its peak
Swearing, yelling, saying something hurtful, storming out, going silent
Outcomes
The result of your anger response
Scaring people around you, damaging a relationship, feeling guilty afterwards, getting fired

After completing a few entries, ask yourself:

  • Do you notice any patterns in your anger triggers? Are certain situations, people, or times of day more likely to set it off?
  • Which warning signs best indicate that your anger is growing — and which appear earliest, while you still have time to intervene?
  • What coping skills could you use to stop the anger from escalating? (The Four Ds worksheet can help with this.)
