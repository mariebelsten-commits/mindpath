How to use this worksheet

Start by choosing a goal that matters to you — something you have been avoiding or putting off. Write down what "done" looks like at step 10, then figure out where you are today. Fill in the steps in between. You don't need to plan everything at once — focus on just the next one or two steps, schedule them, and build from there.

1
Set Your Goal

Choose a goal that is personally meaningful to you — something you have been avoiding, or that feels left undone and is affecting how you feel. Think about what you're not doing that, if you tackled it, would make a real difference.

Dealing with unopened post Starting to exercise Sorting a cluttered room Reconnecting with a friend Looking for a new job Sorting finances
↓
2
Where Are You Now?

Before you map your steps, work out where you are starting from. Step 1 is "made no progress at all" and step 10 is "goal fully achieved." Where does your situation sit right now?

Tip Things may not be quite as bad as step 1. Even a small amount of progress — or a situation that could be worse — might mean you are already at step 2 or 3. Be honest, but also be fair to yourself.

4
Make It SMART

A goal is much more likely to be achieved if it is SMART. Work through each prompt below, focusing on your first one or two steps — the ones you are going to tackle this week.

SSpecific — not vague
MMeasurable — you'll know when it's done
AAchievable — it will achieve what you hope
RRealistic — within your power to do it
TTime-limited — you know exactly when you'll do it
↓
5
Activity Schedule

Schedule your next steps into the coming week. Enter the day, the activity (which step you'll tackle), the time you'll do it, and — once you've done it — fill in the consequences row: what changed in your thoughts, feelings, actions, or impact on others.

Remember You don't need to plan all five days now. Start with just the first step or two. After completing each one, fill in the "Consequences" row — this reflection is an important part of breaking the avoidance cycle.

6
Reflect on Your Progress

After completing your first step or two, take a moment to reflect. What did the experience teach you? Did the reality match the fear you had about doing it?

Questions to consider Was the step as hard as you expected? Did your worry about doing it match what actually happened? What have you learned about how you deal with tasks you've been avoiding?
🪜 Remember: You don't have to climb the whole staircase today. One step at a time is enough. Each step you complete is evidence that you can do it — and the next step will feel a little easier because of it.
