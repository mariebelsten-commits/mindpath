Forgiveness

Research shows that people who forgive are happier and healthier than those who hold resentments. These steps can help you find a way forward.

What is forgiveness?

Forgiveness is the act of untying ourselves from the thoughts and feelings that tie us to an offence. It is not the same as forgetting — the offending act may remain part of our story. But forgiving can lessen the event's grip on us and help us move away from the role of victim.

An important premise is to accept that we are all — at times — imperfect, weak, self-interested, and likely to hurt others. If we can accept that, there is less to be permanently devastated about. There is no magic formula for forgiveness, but the steps below offer some direction.

1
What They Did

Identify exactly what the other person did that caused you pain and hurt. Be specific — name the action, the words, or the behaviour rather than speaking in generalities.

↓
2
The Effect on You

Acknowledge what the experience did to you. This step asks you to look honestly at the full emotional and psychological impact — not to dwell in it, but to see it clearly.

❤️ How it made me feel
💭 Thoughts surrounding the incident
💡 What it meant to me
🔘 Buttons it pressed for me
↓
3
Your Own Responsibility

Consider honestly whether you carry any responsibility for part of what happened. This is not about self-blame, and it is not always applicable — sometimes we are simply wronged. But where responsibility does exist on both sides, naming your part can be freeing.

↓
4
Understanding the Other Person

Consider the other person's situation, intentions, and human weaknesses. Understanding is not the same as condoning. But seeing them as a complex, fallible human — rather than simply as someone who wronged you — can loosen the hold of resentment.

Questions to guide you What might have been going on in their life at the time? Do you think they intended harm, or were they thoughtless or careless? What vulnerabilities or wounds of their own might have driven this? Is there any part of this they may not have understood?
↓
5
What You Cannot Forgive

Be honest about this. There may be aspects of the behaviour that remain, for now, outside the reach of your forgiveness. Acknowledging this is not failure — it is part of the process. You do not have to force forgiveness you do not feel.

↓
6
Deciding to Forgive

Based on your reflections above, consider whether forgiveness feels possible or appropriate. If yes, you might choose to mark that decision in some way — a deliberate act of letting go can help make it feel real.

A ceremony of letting go One approach: write everything down — your anger, your pain, your resentment — in a letter or on a sheet of paper. Then burn it, or tear it up, or close it in a box. The act of physically releasing it can help move the decision from your head into your body.
🕊️ Remember: Forgiving does not mean what happened was acceptable. It does not mean you must trust that person again, or stay in a relationship with them. It means choosing to free yourself from the weight of carrying it.
