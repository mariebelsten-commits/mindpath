Decatastrophising

'What if' thoughts often expect the worst and create anxiety. This worksheet helps you examine those fears honestly, weigh up the evidence, and arrive at a more balanced — and more accurate — view.

What is catastrophising?

Catastrophising means mentally jumping to the worst possible outcome and treating it as though it's almost certain. 'What if' thoughts are the most common form: "What if I fail? What if people judge me? What if everything falls apart?" These thoughts increase anxiety and make situations feel far more threatening than they really are.

This worksheet helps you slow down, look at the actual evidence, consider more balanced possibilities, and remind yourself that you can cope — even if things don't go perfectly. Changing your thinking takes practice, but it gets easier every time you try.

Challenging your thinking — tips & tricks

  • Look at the evidence — focus on facts, not feelings or opinions. Ask: do I have solid, objective proof? Think like you're in a courtroom — only clear facts count.
  • Find alternative explanations — ask whether there are other ways to interpret this situation that you haven't considered.
  • Consider the advantages and disadvantages — does thinking this way help you reach your goals and feel better, or does it hold you back?
  • Remind yourself you can cope — and view change as a chance for growth and new opportunities, not just as a threat.
