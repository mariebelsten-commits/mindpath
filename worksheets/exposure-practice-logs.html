Exposure Practice & Learning Logs

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Exposure works by helping your brain learn that feared outcomes are less likely — or more manageable — than anxiety predicts. These logs guide you through planning each exposure, recording what happened session by session, and reflecting on what you learned. Used consistently, they build compelling evidence that anxiety decreases with practice.

📋 Plan

Set a clear target, identify safety behaviours to drop, and prepare your coping supports before you begin

📝 Log Each Session

Record your predictions, what actually happened, and your SUDS ratings before, during, and after each exposure

💡 Learn and Reflect

After each exposure, capture the key learning — what the experience tells you about anxiety and your ability to cope

📊 Track Progress

Use the multi-session tracking log to see patterns over time and build evidence that anxiety reduces with repeated practice

How to Use These Logs

Work through all four parts in order. Parts 1 and 2 are completed before and during each exposure session. Parts 3 and 4 are completed afterwards. Bring your completed logs to your therapy sessions to review progress together.

1

Graded Exposure Planner

Before starting exposure work, identify your target fear, what you predict will happen, and which safety behaviours you will aim to reduce. Prepare your coping supports in advance so you have a plan ready when anxiety rises.

2

Exposure Session Log

For each individual session, record your predicted fear level, your task plan, and safety behaviours you will drop. During the session, note your peak fear. Afterwards, record your end fear rating and what you noticed about anxiety over time.

3

Exposure Learning Log

After each session, reflect on what actually happened compared to your prediction. The key question is: what did this experience teach you about anxiety, safety, or your ability to cope? This reflection is where the learning is consolidated.

4

Exposure Tracking Log

Use this multi-session table to log each practice over time. Tracking SUDS start, peak, and end across sessions builds visible evidence that anxiety reduces with practice — which is itself a powerful form of learning.

💡 Reminders: Complete each exposure in an environment with minimal distractions. Aim to stay until peak anxiety reduces by at least half, or for a minimum of 10–15 minutes. Do not use distraction or reassurance to escape the feeling — your brain needs to experience coping without safety behaviours for learning to occur.
