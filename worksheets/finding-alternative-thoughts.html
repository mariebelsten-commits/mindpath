How It Works

A step-by-step guide

You don't need to complete every section — focus on the thinking habits that feel most relevant to you right now.

1

Notice the unhelpful thought

When you're feeling anxious, low, or distressed, try to identify the thought that's driving the feeling. What are you telling yourself about the situation?

2

Match it to a thinking habit

Look through the 12 thought patterns below and find the thinking habit that best describes what your mind is doing. You might recognise more than one — that's common.

3

Use the guiding prompts

Read the prompts for that thinking habit slowly. Let them land. They're designed to gently loosen the grip of the unhelpful thought and open up alternative ways of seeing things.

4

Write your alternative thought

In the text box, write your own more balanced, realistic thought. It doesn't need to be wildly positive — just a bit more accurate and a bit kinder than the original.

💡 Tip: Alternative thoughts work best when they feel genuinely believable, not just forced positivity. Aim for "more balanced and realistic" rather than "perfectly happy."

📝 How to use this worksheet

For each thinking habit, read the guiding prompts — then write your own more balanced alternative thought in the box below. Focus on the habits that feel most familiar. You can skip any that don't apply to your current situation.

1
Mental Filter
Guiding prompts

Am I only noticing the bad stuff? Am I filtering out the positives? Am I wearing those gloomy specs? What would be more realistic?

2
Mind-Reading
Guiding prompts

Am I assuming I know what others are thinking? What's the evidence? Those are my own thoughts, not theirs. Is there another, more balanced way of looking at it?

3
Prediction
Guiding prompts

Am I thinking that I can predict the future? How likely is it that that might really happen?

4
Compare & Despair
Guiding prompts

Am I doing that compare and despair thing? What would be a more balanced and helpful way of looking at it?

5
Critical Self
Guiding prompts

There I go — that internal bully's at it again. Would most people who really know me say that about me? Is this something that I am totally responsible for?

6
Shoulds & Musts
Guiding prompts

Am I putting more pressure on myself, setting up expectations of myself that are almost impossible? What would be more realistic?

7
Judgements
Guiding prompts

I'm making an evaluation about the situation or person. It's how I make sense of the world, but that doesn't mean my judgements are always right or helpful. Is there another perspective?

8
Emotional Reasoning
Guiding prompts

Just because it feels bad, doesn't necessarily mean it is bad. My feelings are just a reaction to my thoughts — and thoughts are just automatic brain reflexes.

9
Mountains & Molehills
Guiding prompts

Am I exaggerating the good aspects of others, and putting myself down? Or am I exaggerating the negative and minimising the positives? How would someone else see it? What's the bigger picture?

10
Catastrophising
Guiding prompts

OK, thinking that the worst possible thing will definitely happen isn't really helpful right now. What's most likely to happen?

11
Black & White Thinking
Guiding prompts

Things aren't either totally white or totally black — there are shades of grey. Where is this on the spectrum?

12
Memories
Guiding prompts

This is just a reminder of the past. That was then, and this is now. Even though this memory makes me feel upset, it's not actually happening again right now.

🌿 Well done

Finding alternative thoughts is a skill that gets easier with practice. The more you catch and challenge unhelpful thinking habits, the less automatic power they have over how you feel. Keep this worksheet and return to it whenever you need it.
