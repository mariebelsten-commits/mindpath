My Sensory Toolkit

Our senses are our most direct connection to comfort and calm in the present moment. This worksheet helps you build a personalised inventory of the sights, sounds, smells, tastes, textures, and activities that genuinely soothe and restore you.

🧠

Beyond the Classic Five

We have more than five senses — balance, body awareness, and internal state awareness can all be deliberately engaged to reduce distress.

🛡️

A Self-Soothing Tool

Deliberately engaging the senses is one of the most effective distress tolerance strategies, especially when feelings become intense.

📝

Deeply Personal

What soothes one person may not soothe another. Your sensory toolkit is unique — built entirely from things that genuinely work for you.

⏱️

Build Now, Use Later

Create your toolkit when you're calm, so that when distress rises and thinking becomes harder, you have a ready-made list to turn to.

How It Works

A step-by-step guide

Work through each sense category and list as many items as you can — aim for 10–20 per category. The more specific you are, the more useful your toolkit will be.

1

Understand what you're looking for

For each sense category, you're looking for things that bring you genuine pleasure, enjoyment, or comfort. These could be everyday things (a cup of tea, birdsong) or more deliberate experiences (a massage, a favourite piece of music). There are no right or wrong answers — only what actually works for you.

2

Think beyond the classic five senses

We actually have more than five senses. Proprioception (body awareness) and vestibular (balance and movement) respond well to stretching, rocking, dancing, or weighted blankets. Interoception — awareness of internal body states — can be engaged through warmth, slow breathing, and staying hydrated. Include these kinds of activities in your Activity & Movement category.

3

Be as specific as possible

Instead of "music", write the specific genre, artist, or track. Instead of "nature", write "watching waves from a cliff". The more specific your entries, the more likely you are to actually use them when distress is high and decision-making feels hard.

4

Aim for variety across distress levels

Some items work when distress is low (reading a novel, listening to music). Others work when distress is very high (cold water on your face, intense exercise, a weighted blanket). Try to include items that work across a range of distress levels so your toolkit is useful at any point on the SUDS scale.

5

Use your toolkit when distress rises

Sensory self-soothing is especially effective at SUDS 5–6 (moderate to strong distress). When you feel distress building, open your toolkit, choose something from a relevant category, and engage with it fully and mindfully — not as a distraction, but as a deliberate act of self-care.

Our Sensory Systems — An Overview
Sense Located in What it does
👁️ Visual (sight)
retina
Provides information about objects and people.
👂 Auditory (hearing)
inner ear
Provides information about sounds in the environment.
👃 Olfactory (smell)
nose
Provides information about different types of smell.
👅 Gustatory (taste)
tongue
Provides information about different types of taste.
🤚 Tactile (touch)
skin
Tells us about pain, pressure, heat, and object qualities. Has protective and discriminative functions.
🤸 Vestibular (balance)
inner ear
Provides information about where our body is in space and whether we or our surroundings are moving.
💪 Proprioception (body awareness)
joints, muscles, ligaments
Provides information about body position and gauges the force needed to do activities.
🫀 Interoception (internal state)
internal organs
Provides information about the state of our internal organs — hunger, thirst, temperature, heartbeat.

For each category, list things that bring you genuine pleasure, enjoyment, or comfort — not things you think you should enjoy, but things that actually work for you. Be as specific as you can. The more detailed your entries, the more useful your toolkit will be when you need it.

Foods and drinks that are genuinely comforting or pleasurable

🤚 Touch

Textures, temperatures, and physical sensations that soothe
