Developing Compassionate Self Talk

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Compassionate self talk means speaking to yourself with the same warmth, patience, and understanding that you would offer to someone you care about. This worksheet helps you practise recognising critical inner voices and developing gentler, more helpful ways to respond.

👁️ Awareness

Learn to notice the tone and content of your inner voice — especially when things feel difficult or go wrong

🔄 Reframing

Transform harsh self-critical thoughts into compassionate alternatives — the way a caring friend would speak to you

🌿 Soothing Language

Build a personal library of grounding phrases that remind your nervous system it is safe and cared for

💬 Practice

Compassionate self talk is a skill — regular practice makes gentler, more helpful self-talk feel more natural over time

How to Use This Worksheet

Work through each section in order. There are no right or wrong answers — the goal is honest reflection and gentle practice. You can complete this on your own or with your therapist.

1

Notice Your Inner Voice

Begin by simply observing how you currently speak to yourself when things go wrong. Write down the kinds of words or phrases you tend to use — without judgement. Awareness is the first step.

2

The Impact of Critical Self Talk

Reflect on how your inner critic affects your feelings, confidence, and motivation. Understanding the real cost of self-criticism helps build the motivation to change it.

3

Building Compassionate Alternatives

Take each critical thought from Step 1 and rewrite it as a compassionate response — the kind of thing a kind friend, mentor, or therapist might say to you in that moment.

4

Using Soothing Language

Choose or write a few gentle phrases you can use when you feel stressed or overwhelmed. Saying these aloud or writing them down can help regulate your nervous system.

5

Reflection

Notice how it feels to speak to yourself more kindly. Over time, this reflection helps you track progress and deepen your self-compassion practice.

💡 Remember: Self-compassion is not self-indulgence or making excuses. It is treating yourself with the same basic kindness you would offer to anyone who was struggling. Research shows that compassionate self talk reduces anxiety, improves resilience, and supports lasting wellbeing.

My Compassionate Self Talk Worksheet

Complete This Section

Work through each section in order. Be honest about your current self-talk — and be curious rather than self-critical as you explore it.

1. Notice Your Inner Voice

Think about how you usually talk to yourself when things go wrong or feel difficult. What tone or words do you often use?

Example: "I always mess things up"  ·  "I should be stronger"  ·  "No one else would make this mistake"
2. The Impact of Critical Self Talk

Reflect on how these messages affect you:

  • How do these messages make you feel about yourself?
  • How do they affect your motivation, confidence, or relationships?
3. Building Compassionate Alternatives

Imagine how a caring friend, mentor, or therapist might respond to you in those moments.

  • What words would they use?
  • What tone would they speak with?
"You are learning, and it is okay to make mistakes."
"You are trying your best in a hard situation."
"You deserve kindness, not criticism."

Now rewrite your own examples from Step 1 using a compassionate voice:

Critical Self Talk Compassionate Response
I cannot do anything right Everyone makes mistakes. I can learn and try again
4. Using Soothing Language

Try using gentle phrases that remind your nervous system you are safe and cared for. You can write these down or say them aloud when you feel stressed.

I am allowed to slow down I am doing the best I can with what I know I can speak to myself kindly even when I am struggling I can be patient with myself as I grow

5. Reflection
  • How does it feel to speak to yourself in a more compassionate way?
  • What changes do you notice in your mood or body when you do this regularly?
🌿 Remember: Compassionate self talk is a practice, not a performance. Some days it will feel easy; other days it will feel forced or unfamiliar. That is completely normal. The simple act of noticing your inner voice — and gently choosing a kinder response — is already an act of self-compassion.
