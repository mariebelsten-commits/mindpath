How Emotional Sequencing Works

Our feelings do not arise directly from events — they arise from how we interpret those events. The same situation can make one person depressed and another person merely disappointed, depending on what they tell themselves about it. Change the thought, and you change the feeling.

1
Event
Something happens in your life — a situation, interaction, or experience
↓
2
Thoughts — your interpretation
You make sense of what happened. These interpretations are often automatic, distorted, and more negative than the evidence warrants
↓
3
Feelings
Intense or distorted emotions arise from the irrational interpretation — depression, panic, shame, rage
Challenge the thoughts ↓
4
Challenge
Replace distorted interpretations with rational, evidence-based alternatives
↓
5
New Feelings
More proportionate emotions — still real and valid, but no longer distorted or overwhelming

📖 Worked example — how it looks in practice

EVENT: Girlfriend breaks the news that she wants to end the relationship.
Irrational thoughts
"I can't stand living without her."
"I must be a worthless person."
"I'll never find a partner whom I will make happy."
"She knew me and rejected me — there must be something seriously wrong with me."
"I'm too old to find another girlfriend. I'll be lonely for the rest of my life."
Distortions: Catastrophising · All-or-nothing · Jumping to conclusions · Personalising · Focusing on negatives
😔 Current feeling: Depression
Rational challenges
"It's painful right now — but I can stand it."
"There is no evidence that I am a worthless person."
"That's predicting the future. I've been happy before and I'm still the same person."
"There's no evidence anything is seriously wrong — we were just different."
"There are plenty of people my age in the same situation. Being alone for a while might even be good."
🌱 New feeling: Sadness
"I feel sad because I miss her and all the good times we had together. But I will get over these feelings — they won't last forever."

How to use this worksheet

Think about a situation that is currently causing you distress. Work through each step in sequence — from the event to your thoughts and feelings, then through the challenge to new feelings. Take your time with Step 5: identifying and challenging your thoughts is where the real change happens.

1
The Event

Describe the situation that is causing you distress. Be factual and specific — stick to what actually happened, not your interpretation of it.

↓
2
Your Irrational Thoughts

What did you tell yourself about what happened? Write down all the thoughts, interpretations, assumptions, and conclusions that went through your mind. Don't censor them — write them exactly as they appeared.

↓
3
Your Current Feelings

What emotions are those thoughts producing? Name them honestly. These are the feelings that arise from your irrational interpretation — before you challenge it.

Now challenge your thinking ↓
4
Identify the Cognitive Distortions

Look back at the thoughts you wrote in Step 2. Which of these patterns of distorted thinking can you spot? Tick all that apply.

↓
5
Challenge Your Irrational Thoughts

Go back to each thought from Step 2 and write a rational, evidence-based challenge to it. Ask yourself: What is the actual evidence for and against this thought? Am I catastrophising? What would I say to a close friend who thought this about themselves?

↓
6
Your New Feelings

Having challenged your irrational thoughts, what do you feel now — or what might you feel if you genuinely believed the rational alternative? Notice how the feeling often shifts from something overwhelming to something more proportionate and manageable.

🔄 Remember: The goal is not to feel nothing — it is to feel something proportionate. Moving from depression to sadness, or from panic to concern, is a real and meaningful shift. Feelings based on distorted thinking overwhelm us. Feelings that match reality are manageable.
