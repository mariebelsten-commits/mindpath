Overcoming Avoidance

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Avoidance is one of the most powerful ways anxiety maintains itself. When we avoid what frightens us, we get short-term relief — but at a long-term cost. This worksheet helps you understand your own cycle of avoidance, examine what it is really costing you, and use the FACE framework to begin approaching what you have been avoiding.

⚠️ Anxiety

A situation triggers an anxious response:

  • Thoughts: fixation on worst-case scenarios, doubts about ability to cope
  • Physical: shallow breathing, racing heart, shaking, sweating
  • Feelings: dread, irritability, restlessness

🚪 Avoidance

You avoid the trigger to escape the uncomfortable symptoms:

  • You cancel a holiday due to fear of flying
  • You use alcohol to "loosen up" before socialising
  • You procrastinate on an important task

📈 Intensification

You associate avoidance with feeling better, so next time anxiety is worse and avoidance even more tempting:

  • You give up on ever taking a holiday
  • You use more alcohol to socialise
  • You start doing poorly at work due to rushed projects

☁️ Relief

Avoidance lessens the discomfort — but the relief is temporary and comes at a cost:

  • You miss out on your dream holiday
  • You come to rely on alcohol to socialise
  • You have to rush an important task due to procrastinating

How to Use This Worksheet

This worksheet has three parts. Start by mapping your own cycle of avoidance. Then examine the real costs of your avoidance strategies. Finally, use the FACE framework to plan how to begin approaching what you have been avoiding.

1

Map Your Cycle of Avoidance

Identify your specific anxiety trigger and trace how it moves through the four stages: anxiety response, avoidance, temporary relief, and intensification over time.

2

Examine the Costs

List every strategy you have ever used to avoid or escape unpleasant thoughts and feelings. Then ask yourself honestly: did these strategies work long term? What did they cost you?

F

Find Your Target

Identify what you would like to be able to FACE, rather than avoid. Write a list of targets, then choose one that is not too distressing to start with.

A

Action Planning

Decide on the steps you need to take. Break the goal down into manageable steps — like rungs on a ladder — and take them one at a time, starting at the bottom.

C

Coping Strategies

Write down all your coping strategies and identify which will be most useful for this situation.

E

Evaluate

After attempting each step, review how it went. What went well? What didn't? What would you do differently next time?

💡 Remember: The goal is not to eliminate anxiety — it is to stop letting anxiety dictate what you do and don't do. Each time you approach rather than avoid, you teach your brain that the situation is survivable and that you are capable of coping.
