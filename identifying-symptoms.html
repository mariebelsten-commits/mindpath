Identifying Symptoms

Understanding your symptoms is the first step towards managing them. Use this worksheet to map out how anxiety or distress shows up in your body, thoughts, emotions, and behaviour — and to identify the triggers that set things off.

Why this matters

Stress and anxiety don't stay in your head — they show up across every part of your experience. Your body tenses, your thoughts race, your emotions shift, and your behaviour changes. When you can recognise these patterns as symptoms rather than facts or personal failings, you gain the power to respond rather than react.

Work through each section honestly, even the parts that feel uncomfortable. There are no right or wrong answers — this is about understanding yourself more clearly.

"You can't manage what you don't understand. Naming your symptoms is a quiet act of courage."
Part A — Your Symptoms
1
Physical Symptoms
Why managing physical symptoms matters Stress and fear often cause changes in breathing and muscle tension, which can lead to aches, fatigue, and increased feelings of panic. Though anxiety is psychological, it often shows up physically — and for many people, the physical symptoms feel more intense than the emotional ones. Think about how your body reacts to stress: do you tense up? Get headaches or sore shoulders? Controlled breathing and muscle relaxation are simple but powerful tools for managing these sensations.
Common examples
Racing heart Shortness of breath Muscle tension Headaches Fatigue Nausea Sweating Trembling Dizziness Chest tightness Shallow breathing Stomach knots
2
Cognitive Symptoms
What are cognitive symptoms? Cognitive symptoms are the thoughts, images, and mental experiences that arise when you are struggling. These can include racing or intrusive thoughts, difficulty concentrating, negative self-talk, and unwanted mental images or urges that cause distress. Obsessive symptoms — recurring thoughts, urges, or images that feel impossible to switch off — are a form of cognitive symptom. The mind tries to suppress or neutralise them, which often makes them more persistent.
Common examples
Racing thoughts Intrusive thoughts Difficulty concentrating Negative self-talk Worrying Memory problems Catastrophising Indecisiveness Unwanted images Rumination

Behavioural Symptoms
What are behavioural symptoms? Behavioural symptoms are the things you do — or stop doing — when you're struggling. These can include avoidance, withdrawal, and compulsive behaviours. Compulsive symptoms involve feeling driven to perform repetitive actions (such as checking, washing, counting, or seeking reassurance) to reduce anxiety or prevent a feared outcome. While these behaviours provide short-term relief, they often maintain anxiety over time by reinforcing the idea that the feared situation is genuinely dangerous.
Common examples
Avoiding situations Withdrawing from others Checking Seeking reassurance Repetitive actions Over-preparing Sleep changes Eating changes Procrastinating Keeping busy to distract
