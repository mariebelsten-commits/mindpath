How to Use This Worksheet

Work through each step in order. Rather than just talking about unhelpful beliefs, you will actively test them in real life — gathering direct evidence for or against what you think is true. Think of yourself as a scientist, and your thoughts as hypotheses.

1

Identify the Belief

Name the thought or assumption you want to test. Be as specific and honest as possible — vague beliefs are harder to examine. A good belief statement is specific, behavioural, and falsifiable.

2

Rate Your Belief

Record how strongly you currently believe this thought on a scale of 0–100%. This baseline is essential — you will return to it in Step 6.

3

Design the Experiment

Plan the specific action you will take to test your belief. A well-designed experiment is safe, realistic, and directly relevant to the belief you identified. Discuss your plan with your therapist before proceeding.

4

Predict What Will Happen

Write down exactly what you expect to occur. Use specific, observable terms. This prediction captures your belief in action and gives you something concrete to compare against the actual outcome.

5

Record the Outcome

After carrying out the experiment, record exactly what happened as objectively as possible. Set aside interpretations for now and focus on observable facts.

6

Reflect and Re-Rate

Compare what you predicted with what actually happened, then update your belief rating. Even a small shift in percentage represents a meaningful change in how your mind processes evidence.

🧪 Key Principle: The more specific your prediction, the more useful your experiment. Precise predictions are easier to confirm or disconfirm with real evidence. Never design an experiment that puts you or others at risk — always consult your therapist if you are uncertain.
